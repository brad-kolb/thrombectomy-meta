Dear Editors,

We respectfully submit our manuscript, **"Worse Prognosis, Larger Benefit: A Bayesian Hierarchical Ordinal Meta-analysis of 30 Thrombectomy Trials,"** for consideration as an **Original Investigation** in *JAMA Neurology*.

**The clinical question.** This study asks whether there is a continuous gradient between trial-level baseline prognosis under medical therapy alone and the magnitude of treatment benefit from mechanical thrombectomy.

**What we did and found.** We applied a Bayesian hierarchical ordinal regression model to the complete 90-day modified Rankin Scale distributions from all 30 published randomized thrombectomy trials (n = 8,100). The model directly estimates the trial-level correlation between control-group outcomes and treatment effect magnitude. We found a consistent inverse relationship (ρ = −0.51; 95% CrI −0.80 to −0.11): trials enrolling populations with worse expected outcomes under medical therapy showed larger relative thrombectomy benefits. No evidence for a floor effect was identified within the range of enrolled populations: the poorer the control-group outcome, the larger the benefit from mechanical thrombectomy, on average. 

**Why this matters for *JAMA Neurology*'s readership.** Stroke is the leading cause of acquired disability in adults. The selection paradigm for thrombectomy — who is "too sick" or "too well" — affects tens of thousands of patients annually and is actively debated by neurologists and neurosurgeons at every stroke center. This analysis provides a randomized-evidence quantification of the prognosis-benefit gradient and a mechanistic account of why the recent guideline expansions are appropriate.
